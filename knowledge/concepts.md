Photosynthesis: Green plants make their own food using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide. It happens mainly in the leaves, in green parts that contain chlorophyll. The plant takes in carbon dioxide from the air through tiny pores called stomata and water from the soil through roots. Using sunlight energy, it converts these into glucose (food) and releases oxygen. Word equation: carbon dioxide + water --(sunlight, chlorophyll)--> glucose + oxygen.

Water cycle: Water keeps moving between the earth and the sky in a cycle. The sun heats water in rivers, lakes, and oceans, turning it into vapour (evaporation). The vapour rises, cools, and forms tiny droplets that make clouds (condensation). When droplets get heavy they fall as rain, hail, or snow (precipitation). This water flows back into rivers and oceans (collection), and the cycle repeats.

Gravity: Gravity is the force that pulls objects towards each other. The earth's gravity pulls everything towards its centre, which is why things fall down and why we stay on the ground. Heavier-feeling things have more weight because gravity pulls more on more mass. The same force keeps the moon orbiting the earth and the planets orbiting the sun.

States of matter: Matter exists mainly in three states — solid, liquid, and gas. Solids have a fixed shape and volume because their particles are tightly packed. Liquids have a fixed volume but take the shape of their container because particles can move around. Gases have neither fixed shape nor volume and spread out to fill any space. Heating can change a solid to a liquid (melting) and a liquid to a gas (evaporation/boiling); cooling reverses these.

Fractions: A fraction shows a part of a whole, written as numerator over denominator, like 3/4. The denominator (bottom) tells how many equal parts the whole is divided into; the numerator (top) tells how many parts we take. Equivalent fractions name the same amount, e.g. 1/2 = 2/4 = 4/8. To add fractions with the same denominator, add the numerators and keep the denominator.

Parts of speech: English words are grouped by their job in a sentence. A noun names a person, place, animal, or thing (Ram, school, tiger, pen). A verb shows an action or state (run, is, eat). An adjective describes a noun (tall, red, happy). A pronoun replaces a noun (he, she, it, they). An adverb tells how, when, or where an action happens (quickly, today, here).

Solar system: The solar system has the sun at its centre and eight planets going around it: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. The planets closest to the sun (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars) are small and rocky. The next four are large gas giants. The earth is the only planet known to support life because it has air, water, and the right temperature.

Food chain: A food chain shows how energy passes from one living thing to another through eating. It starts with producers (green plants) that make their own food. Herbivores (plant-eaters) eat the plants, and carnivores (meat-eaters) eat other animals. For example: grass -> grasshopper -> frog -> snake -> eagle. The sun is the original source of energy for every food chain.

Electricity basics: An electric current is the flow of charge through a conductor like a copper wire. A simple circuit needs a source (a cell or battery), connecting wires, and a device such as a bulb. The circuit must be closed (complete loop) for current to flow and the bulb to glow; if there is a gap (open circuit), it stops. A switch is used to open or close the circuit.

Acids and bases: Acids taste sour (like lemon and tamarind) and turn blue litmus paper red. Bases taste bitter and feel soapy (like baking soda and soap) and turn red litmus paper blue. When an acid and a base react together they cancel each other out and form salt and water — this is called neutralisation. The pH scale measures how acidic or basic something is, from 0 to 14, where 7 is neutral.

Air and its composition: Air is a mixture of gases. About 78 percent is nitrogen and about 21 percent is oxygen; the rest is carbon dioxide, water vapour, and small amounts of other gases. Living things need oxygen to breathe, and plants need carbon dioxide for photosynthesis. Air also has weight and takes up space, which is why it can push things and fill a balloon.
